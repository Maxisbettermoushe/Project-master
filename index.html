My diaganoses

Johanessburg, South Africa

  • I was diagnosed with Type One diabetes on August 2nd 2004, just twenty two days before my fourth birthday. Due the rampant poverty of South Africa the healthcare system wasnâ€™t the best, but fornatantly my family was fairly well off so I had the best available care. When I was diagnosed I was given the prognosis of a 30 year life span.

Process of learning how to be diabetic

Johanessburg, South Africa

  • My life completely changed after I was diagnosed, as the amount of food I could eat, when I could eat and injections became a daily hassle. To this day when I recall my memories of South Africa, I remember how my mother chasing me down to jab me with syringes of insulin or how I was devastated by not being able to eat the cake at birthday parties because I had already consumed my daily allotment of carbohydrates. The transition into being a diabetic was confusing as I too young to fully comprehend what it fully meant. All I knew was that I hated being Diabetic.

First Pump

Johanessburg, South Africa

  • Fortunately, before I moved to England in 2005. My doctor recommended that I was to be placed on a diabetic pump, as at this time, they were just starting to become commercially available yet in England the M.H.R.A. (British F.D.A.) only allowed adults access to pumps. The doctor recommended me to get a pump since they were easier to manage and less intrusive compared to injections.

Growing up

Manchester, England

  • I spent the majority of my life growing up in England, which in turn shape my feelings on being diabetic. I often viewed being diabetic with a negative connotation. As I would often not be allowed to go on school trips or sleepovers as parents and teachers didn't understand how to take care of my Diabetes. Yet, the relationship I built through the time I spent at diabetic camps and hospital events allowed me to realize that there were many actual benefits. The introduction to research studies while in England ignited my passion for science and technology. This passion has developed into my desire to become a doctor and major in both computer science and biology.

Make A Wish

Lapland, Finland

  • Being a member of the Royal Manchester Children's Hospital, I was given the option to attend a Make-A-Wish fundraiser in Lapland, Finland. Without a doubt, the trip was one of the most impactful events of my life, as for once, it allowed me to feel as if I was normal. My relationship with Diabetes up to this point was often negative, yet this trip made me realize that I should be thankful that while it is an incurable disease, it is manageable. Without being diabetic, I would have never experienced such an amazing trip or meet so many new people.

Bullying

Manchester, England

  • Throughout my experience of being a diabetic, I can recall several times where I was singled out for being Diabetic. I can remember the disappointment of not getting invited for sleepovers because parents were afraid to manage a diabetic kid or the time I hid in the bathroom stall when the boys at soccer camp made fun of me because I had to stop playing and adjust my sugar level. The most shocking was the time I was finished when I went through security at an international airport because of suspicions about the electronic device attached to my body (my pump). These events came to form my negative connotation of being diabetic.

Sensor

Manchester, England

  • While living in England, technology for people with Diabetes improved immensely. A device called a Sensor or Continous Glucose Monitor (CGM), which monitors your glucose levels and prematurely warns of Hypoglycemia and Hyperglycemia, was released to the public for the first time. I was fascinated by how new technology could potentially save thousands of Diabetes from a trip to the hospital. In turn, this lead me to want to help explore new technology through participation in research studies.

Think Tank- Study

Chester, England

  • Throughout my childhood, I took part in several studies for adolescent diabetics. I loved interacting with other kids with Diabetes and comparing their experiences against my own. I strong attribute the change in my beliefs on being diabetic to these studies. Being diabetic, afforded me opportunities to interact with the medical world and connected with a large community. Looking back now, these minor studies leaving me feeling thankful for how Diabetes had lead me on to my path towards becoming a doctor.

What England Taught Me

England

  • England taught me that being Diabetic would be difficult, and that I may always stuck with the chores that come with it. Yet, I shouldn't dwell on all of the negative but be thankful for all the opportunities it has afforded me. My love for science and technology would like to have never develope if I was never diagnosed. The friends and experiences I enjoyed would have dramatically changed. I would not be the same person I am today if weren't for my diagnosis. England made me realize what being diabetic meant to me.

Independance

Belmont, Massachuessetts

  • I moved to Belmont, Massachusetts, for high school and college. At this point, my understanding of diabetes meant that I primarily took control of managing my care. This allowed me to mature and gain a sense of independence. Understanding that I must be wary of what I eat and track my supplies for the first time gave me confidence to become my own person

Inque

Lowell, Massachuessetts

  • OIt is recommended that all diabetic have some form of identification in case of an emergency. Typically people have medical alert jewelry. When I was younger tried wearing necklaces and brackets that states that I was type one diabetic, but I would often fiddle, break, or lose them. About three years ago, during my diabetic doctor's appointment, I was asked if an intern could run the appointment. The intern asked me what form of identification I had for being type one in case of an emergency. I explained that I wasn't a fan of wearing the bracelets or necklaces. The intern then asked if I had thought of possibly getting a tattoo. I was instantly hooked on the idea of getting a tattoo. When research into getting a tattoo, I was fortunate to make a connection with a tattoo company called Inque, which a revolutionary company that is creating a healthier and easier method to create and remove tattoos. Through this connection, I have become a spokesperson for Diabetes awareness and hope to raise awareness of the importance of having medical identification. For more information, look at https://inquealert.com/ and https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2019/11/12/1945479/0/en/INQUE-Disrupts-Tattoo-Industry-with-Safer-Ink-That-Can-Be-Turned-Off-in-One-Session.html.

Studys and Research

Boston, Massachuessetts

  • Just as I had in England, I took part in a multitude of research studies in America for type one diabetes. My love for science and technology only grew as I saw cutting edge technology first hand. I was a part of the groundbreaking Medtronic 670G pump trails, which was an opportunity to test the first automatic insulin delivery device, which completely changed how I managed my diabetic care. The opportunities I have gained by being diabetic has changed my views. I still hate the fact that I was diagnosed and have come to terms with the fact that I will always be a type one diabetic, yet I now understand that there is a lot of positive things that have come out of it. I'm grateful for how it has affected me and am proud to be a type one diabetic.

Future

Amherst, Massachuessetts

  • Throughout my journey as a type one diabetic, my admiration for the scientific and medical world has only grown. The fact that our understanding of technology and science can make life easier for those who need it astonishes me. This admiration has lead me to Umass Amherst, as a Pre-med student majoring in Biology and Computer Science. As I desire to help give back to the communities that have supported me.
